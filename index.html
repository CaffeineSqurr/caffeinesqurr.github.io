Elliot Branson

Computer Science PhD. student with MARS Lab at the University of Minnesota

I'm interested in how technology can help people do what they want with as little distraction as possible. Robotics has always been one aspect of the intersection between computers and the physical world. In robotics the goal is not only to sense the world but to interact with it.

I study robotic navigation, computer vision, and other estimation problems. Away from computers I'm most likely to be found climbing, cooking, and traveling.

Labs

Multiple Autonomous Robotic Systems (MARS) Laboratory

The Multiple Autonomous Robotic Systems (MARS) Laboratory is a multi-disciplinary research lab at the University of Minnesota. The objective of the MARS Lab is to promote basic research and education in robotics and computer vision with special emphasis on estimation and control of autonomous ground, aerial, and space vehicles.

Research is focused on Mobile robot localization, mapping, navigation, and tracking; Sensor Networks; Distributed Sensing and Control; Vision-aided Inertial Navigation; Structure from Motion; and Human-Robot Interaction.

Robotic Sensor Networks (RSN) Lab

The Robotic Sensor Networks Lab focuses on algorithmic and systems aspects of Robotic Sensor Networks (RSNs). Broadly speaking, an RSN is a network of devices equipped with sensing, communication and actuation capabilities. Designing algorithms for robotic sensor networks requires tackling challenging problems that lie at the intersection of robotics, perception and communication. Our group works on a broad set of RSN related problems ranging from theoretical problems such as Pursuit Evasion and Sensor Placement to developing systems in which robots act as Robotic Routers or Data Mules. In recent years, we have been focusing on Environmental Monitoring applications.

Quadrotor VINS

By combining a VINS (Vision-Aided Inertial Navigation System) with a cheap Parrot AR.Drone quad-rotor we are able to precisely locate a fast moving vehicle operating in three dimensions without a GPS.

Visual Place Recognition

People are able to recognize places they've previously been from a single photograph. This project seeks to allow low cost devices equipped with a camera identify if/when they return to the same location in real time.

Binary Image Features

The first step to enable computer programs to reason about images is to extract visual "features". With Binary Features we explore using features, which can be computed quickly and stored efficiently as a series of comparisons.

Carp Tracking

I've worked with an interdisciplinary team from the RSN lab at the University of Minnesota Computer Science Department as well as partners from the Department of Fisheries, Wildlife, and Conservation Biology at the University of Minnesota, Johns Hopkins University, and Central State University in Ohio in a effort to detect, locate, track, and eliminate invasive Chinese carp in Minnesota waterways.

Data Mules

The field of wireless sensor networks has grown in recent years due to its ability to measure a diverse range of inputs. In some applications though it is impractical to completely cover the area, in these situations robots can act as intermediaries carrying the data between sensors. In this project we worked to answer fundamental questions about these hybrid robot sensor networks.
